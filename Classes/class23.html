02 — Motivation

Why Alignment — The Problem with SFT Alone

SFT teaches a model to follow instructions by imitation — it learns to replicate the style and content of responses in the training dataset. But imitation has a fundamental problem: it can't distinguish between two valid responses of different quality. If your dataset contains a mediocre answer and an excellent answer to similar questions, SFT has no mechanism to prefer one over the other.

More critically, SFT has no built-in mechanism to prevent harmful outputs. A model trained purely by SFT will sometimes produce responses that are technically on-topic but still harmful, biased, or dishonest — because it's seen examples of all of these in its training data.

⚖️

Quality Discrimination

SFT treats all correct responses equally. Alignment teaches the model that some responses are meaningfully better than others — more accurate, more complete, safer.

🛡️

Safety & Harmlessness

Alignment reduces the probability of harmful, toxic, or deceptive outputs by directly penalising them via pairwise preference data labelled by humans or strong AI judges.

🎯

Human Preference Matching

What humans consider a "good" answer often can't be captured in instruction data alone. Preference alignment encodes nuanced human judgment at scale.

🔄

Beyond Imitation

RLHF and DPO optimise the model with a signal that can be stronger than any single demonstration — iterative improvement driven by relative comparisons.

The SFT Gap — What's Missing

Consider this situation: you SFT a medical assistant model. Both these responses might appear in your SFT dataset:

✓ Better response
"Ibuprofen and paracetamol work through different mechanisms, so can be safely alternated. Take ibuprofen with food to avoid stomach irritation. However, always consult your doctor for specific dosing advice, especially if you have kidney or liver conditions."
✗ Worse response
"Yes, you can mix ibuprofen and paracetamol. They're both pain relievers and taking them together is fine."

SFT learns from both equally. Preference alignment tells the model: the first response is preferred. This relative signal is the core of alignment training.

03 — Core Concepts

Key Concepts You Need to Know

📖 Key Terminology

Preference Alignment
The process of fine-tuning a model to produce outputs that better match human preferences, as expressed through pairwise comparisons of (chosen, rejected) response pairs.
RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)
A multi-stage pipeline: (1) collect preference data, (2) train a reward model on preferences, (3) use RL (PPO) to optimise the language model against the reward model.
DPO (Direct Preference Optimisation)
A simpler alternative to RLHF that skips the reward model entirely. It directly optimises the LLM on preference pairs using a classification-style loss, achieving similar results with much greater stability.
Reward Model (RM)
A model trained to predict how much a human would prefer a given response. Takes a (prompt, response) pair as input and outputs a scalar reward score. Used in RLHF to generate training signal.
Chosen / Rejected
The two components of a preference pair. For a given prompt, the "chosen" response is the one a human annotator preferred; "rejected" is the one they preferred less.
KL Divergence Penalty
A regularisation term added during RLHF that prevents the aligned model from drifting too far from the SFT reference model. Without it, the model would find degenerate ways to maximise reward (reward hacking).
PPO (Proximal Policy Optimisation)
The RL algorithm used in the RLHF stage to update the language model. Treats the LLM as a policy and the reward model score as the environment signal.
β (Beta) in DPO
The temperature parameter controlling how strongly DPO pushes the model away from the reference model. Higher β = closer to reference model; lower β = more aggressively prefers "chosen" responses.

The Reward Model — Teaching the Model to Judge

In RLHF, before you can optimise the language model, you need something to measure "how good is this response?" That's the reward model. It's trained on the same preference data — pairs of (prompt, chosen, rejected) — using a Bradley-Terry ranking loss:

Reward Model Training Signal
Prompt: "Explain recursion"
→
Response A (Chosen) → r = +2.4
Response B (Rejected) → r = −0.8
→
Loss: −log σ(rchosen − rrejected)
The reward model learns to assign higher scalar scores to chosen responses than rejected ones. After training, it can score any (prompt, response) pair.
💡

Reward model architecture: Typically the same base LLM as the policy model, but with the final language modelling head replaced by a linear layer that outputs a single scalar. For example, if you're aligning Llama-3-8B, your reward model is also Llama-3-8B with a regression head. This ensures the reward model "understands" the same language space as the model it's judging.

Preference Data — The Core Training Signal

All preference alignment methods require the same fundamental input: a dataset of triplets — (prompt, chosen_response, rejected_response). The difference between RLHF and DPO is how they use this data, not what data they need.

chosen
{
  "prompt": "What is photosynthesis?",
  "chosen": "Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert sunlight, water, and CO₂ into glucose and oxygen using chlorophyll. It occurs in two stages: the light-dependent reactions in the thylakoid and the Calvin cycle in the stroma."
}
rejected
{
  "prompt": "What is photosynthesis?",
  "rejected": "Photosynthesis is how plants make food from sunlight."
}
🟢

Key insight about preference data: The "rejected" response doesn't have to be wrong or harmful — it just has to be worse than the "chosen" response. This allows very nuanced quality signals: a complete, well-explained answer vs. an oversimplified one, a safe response vs. a mildly risky one, or a factually precise answer vs. a vague one.

04 — RLHF

RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

RLHF is the technique that powered InstructGPT, ChatGPT, Claude, and most of the major production LLMs. It's a three-stage pipeline that takes a base or SFT model and optimises it using human preference signals via reinforcement learning.

The Three Stages of RLHF

01

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) — The Starting Point

You start with your SFT model from Class 22. This is your policy model — the model you will optimise. You also need a frozen copy of this model (the reference policy) to compute the KL penalty later.

02

Reward Model Training

Collect human preference data: for many prompts, generate multiple responses and have human annotators rank them. Train a reward model (RM) on these pairwise preference labels. The RM learns to assign a scalar score to any (prompt, response) pair — higher = more preferred.

03

RL Fine-Tuning with PPO

Use the reward model as the environment. The language model is the policy. For each prompt, the policy generates a response, the RM scores it, and PPO updates the policy to maximise expected reward — while a KL penalty keeps the policy from diverging from the SFT reference model.

The PPO Objective

During RLHF fine-tuning, the objective being maximised is:

RLHF objective (PPO) 
# At each training step, for a prompt x and generated response y:

# 1. Score the response with the reward model
reward = reward_model(x, y)

# 2. Compute KL divergence from the SFT reference model
kl_penalty = KL(policy_model(y|x) || sft_reference_model(y|x))

# 3. Final objective: maximise reward, penalise divergence from SFT
objective = reward - β * kl_penalty

# PPO clips the policy update to prevent too-large steps:
# L_CLIP = E[min(r_t * A_t, clip(r_t, 1-ε, 1+ε) * A_t)]
# where r_t = π_θ(a|s) / π_θ_old(a|s)  and  A_t = advantage estimate
⚠️

Why is RLHF complex? RLHF requires training three separate models simultaneously — the SFT model, the reward model, and the RL-updated policy — all of which must fit in memory at the same time. PPO is notoriously unstable and hyperparameter-sensitive. This is why DPO (next section) became popular: it achieves similar results without the reward model or RL loop.

05 — DPO

DPO — Direct Preference Optimisation

DPO, introduced by Rafailov et al. (Stanford, 2023), reformulates the RLHF objective into a simple supervised learning problem. The key insight: the optimal RLHF policy has a closed-form solution, and you can directly optimise toward it without ever training a reward model or running a PPO loop.

🧮

No Reward Model

DPO eliminates the need for a separate reward model. The language model itself implicitly learns to assign higher probability to chosen responses.

📉

Simple Loss Function

Just a binary cross-entropy over preference pairs. No RL loop, no PPO, no clipping. Extremely stable to train compared to RLHF.

⚡

Efficient

Only two models in memory (policy + frozen reference), not three. Much lower GPU memory footprint and simpler training infrastructure.

🏆

Competitive Performance

DPO achieves results comparable or superior to PPO-based RLHF on most benchmarks, especially when data quality is high.

The DPO Loss Function

DPO derives from the RLHF objective algebraically and arrives at this closed-form loss:

DPO loss (conceptual) 
# For a prompt x, chosen response y_w, rejected response y_l:

# Log-ratio of policy vs reference model for each response
log_ratio_chosen  = log π_θ(y_w | x) - log π_ref(y_w | x)
log_ratio_rejected = log π_θ(y_l | x) - log π_ref(y_l | x)

# DPO loss: maximise probability of correctly preferring chosen over rejected
L_DPO = -E[ log σ( β * (log_ratio_chosen - log_ratio_rejected) ) ]

# Intuition:
#   Increase probability of chosen response RELATIVE to the reference model
#   Decrease probability of rejected response RELATIVE to the reference model
#   β controls how aggressively to deviate from the reference policy
🔵

What β actually does: β is the KL constraint strength. With β = 0, there's no regularisation and the model will overfit aggressively to the preference data. With very high β, the model barely moves from the SFT reference. In practice, β between 0.1 and 0.5 is typical — lower for aggressive alignment, higher when you want to preserve the SFT model's capabilities.

RLHF vs DPO — Head-to-Head Comparison

Dimension RLHF (PPO) DPO
Models needed SFT + Reward Model + Policy SFT reference + Policy (2 models)
Training stability Unstable — PPO is sensitive to hyperparams Very stable — standard supervised loss
Memory footprint 3× model memory required 2× model memory required
Implementation complexity High — reward model pipeline + RL loop Low — same as SFT with different loss
Online data generation Yes — policy generates new responses each step No — offline, uses fixed preference dataset

Industry adoption (2024–2025) OpenAI, Anthropic (early) Widely preferred for efficiency

🟢

What to use in practice: For most fine-tuning workflows — especially on Colab with limited GPU memory — DPO is the right choice. It's stable, efficient, and achieves excellent alignment results. Only consider full RLHF with PPO if you have the infrastructure to generate online preference data and multiple large-model instances simultaneously.

06 — Preference Data

Preference Datasets — What You Need

Preference data is the fuel of alignment. Unlike SFT data (instruction → response), preference data must always come in triplets: (prompt, chosen, rejected). The quality, diversity, and contrast between chosen and rejected responses determines how well alignment works.

Preference Data Format

Standard DPO dataset format (HuggingFace) 
{
  "prompt": "What are the side effects of aspirin?",

  "chosen": "Common side effects of aspirin include stomach irritation, nausea, and increased bleeding risk. Serious but rare effects include stomach ulcers, allergic reactions, and tinnitus at high doses. People with kidney disease, bleeding disorders, or who are pregnant should consult a doctor before use.",

  "rejected": "Aspirin can cause stomach issues. It's a common over-the-counter drug. Just follow the instructions on the label."
}

Some datasets also include a "messages" format compatible with chat templates:

Chat-format preference data (messages style) 
{
  "chosen": [
    {"role": "user",      "content": "What are the side effects of aspirin?"},
    {"role": "assistant", "content": "Common side effects include stomach irritation..."}
  ],
  "rejected": [
    {"role": "user",      "content": "What are the side effects of aspirin?"},
    {"role": "assistant", "content": "Aspirin can cause stomach issues..."}
  ]
}

How Preference Data Is Collected

Human

Human Annotation

Human annotators compare two model-generated responses for the same prompt and label the preferred one. Gold standard but expensive and slow.

✓ Highest quality signal
✓ Captures nuanced preferences
✓ Domain expert annotations possible
✗ Very expensive at scale
✗ Annotator disagreement / bias
AI Judge

AI-Assisted Labelling

A strong frontier model (GPT-4, Claude) acts as the judge — given two responses, it labels the better one. Scales to millions of pairs cheaply.

✓ Very scalable and cheap
✓ Consistent judging criteria
✓ Can judge in diverse domains
✗ Inherits judge model biases
✗ Self-alignment circularity risk
Synthetic

Synthetic Construction

Generate chosen responses from a strong model and rejected from a weak model. Or intentionally degrade good responses by removing safety qualifiers.

✓ Zero annotation cost
✓ Full control of data distribution
✓ Easy domain specialisation
✗ Chosen/rejected gap may be too obvious
✗ May not reflect real failure modes
📊

Recommended open datasets for DPO: Anthropic/hh-rlhf (human helpfulness & harmlessness pairs), HuggingFaceH4/ultrafeedback_binarized (AI-judged preference pairs across 64K instructions), Intel/orca_dpo_pairs (DPO-ready pairs from GPT-4 vs GPT-3.5), and argilla/dpo-mix-7k (curated 7K high-contrast pairs).

07 — Training Pipeline

DPO Training Pipeline with TRL + Unsloth

The practical implementation of DPO is much simpler than full RLHF. TRL's DPOTrainer handles the reference model management, log-ratio computation, and β-weighted loss automatically. Combined with Unsloth for LoRA efficiency, you can run DPO on an A100 Colab in under an hour.

01

Load SFT Model as Starting Point

DPO starts from your SFT-trained model. Load it with Unsloth for 4-bit quantisation and LoRA. The same model is automatically used as the frozen reference policy inside DPOTrainer.

02

Prepare the Preference Dataset

Load a triplet dataset with "prompt", "chosen", "rejected" columns. Apply the same chat template you used during SFT training so token sequences are consistent between stages.

03

Configure DPOTrainer

Set β (typically 0.1–0.3), learning rate (lower than SFT, ~5e-5), and a smaller number of epochs (1–3). DPO converges faster than SFT because the preference signal is richer per example.

04

Train and Monitor

Monitor chosen_rewards and rejected_rewards — the gap should increase over training (chosen scores rising, rejected scores falling). If they converge, check your β and learning rate.

05

Evaluate and Push to Hub

Evaluate with side-by-side win-rate comparisons against the SFT base. If win-rate > 60%, the alignment is working. Push the DPO adapter separately so you can compose CPT → SFT → DPO adapters.

DPO Setup Code

Python · Unsloth + TRL 
from unsloth import FastLanguageModel
from trl import DPOTrainer, DPOConfig
from datasets import load_dataset

# ── 1. Load SFT model (starting point for DPO) ──────────────────
model, tokenizer = FastLanguageModel.from_pretrained(
    model_name = "your-hf-username/llama3-medical-sft",  # your SFT output
    max_seq_length = 2048,
    load_in_4bit = True,
)

# ── 2. Add LoRA for DPO fine-tuning ─────────────────────────────
model = FastLanguageModel.get_peft_model(
    model,
    r = 16,
    target_modules = ["q_proj", "k_proj", "v_proj", "o_proj"],
    lora_alpha = 16,
    lora_dropout = 0,
    bias = "none",
    use_gradient_checkpointing = "unsloth",
)

# ── 3. Load preference dataset ───────────────────────────────────
dataset = load_dataset("argilla/dpo-mix-7k", split="train")
# Expects columns: "prompt", "chosen", "rejected"

# ── 4. Configure DPO training ────────────────────────────────────
dpo_config = DPOConfig(
    beta = 0.1,               # KL constraint — how far to diverge from SFT ref
    learning_rate = 5e-5,    # Lower than SFT (was 2e-4)
    num_train_epochs = 1,
    per_device_train_batch_size = 2,
    gradient_accumulation_steps = 4,
    warmup_ratio = 0.1,
    output_dir = "./dpo-output",
    logging_steps = 10,
    save_steps = 200,
    bf16 = True,
    max_length = 2048,
    max_prompt_length = 512,
)

# ── 5. Create DPOTrainer ─────────────────────────────────────────
trainer = DPOTrainer(
    model = model,
    ref_model = None,     # Unsloth handles ref model automatically
    args = dpo_config,
    train_dataset = dataset,
    tokenizer = tokenizer,
)

# ── 6. Train ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
trainer.train()

Evaluating Alignment

Unlike SFT where you can use perplexity and exact-match metrics, alignment quality is harder to measure automatically. Here are the most useful evaluation approaches:

Method How It Works Best For
Win-rate (LLM judge) Ask GPT-4 or Claude to compare SFT vs DPO responses for the same prompts. Count how often DPO wins. Overall quality comparison
Reward score tracking Run a separate reward model on outputs. Higher mean reward = better alignment. Watch chosen_rewards ↑ and rejected_rewards ↓ during training. Training monitoring
MT-Bench 80 multi-turn questions scored by GPT-4 on a 1–10 scale. Standard benchmark for chat assistants. Chat quality benchmarking
AlpacaEval 2.0 Win-rate against GPT-4-Turbo baseline on 805 instructions. Length-controlled to penalise verbose responses. Helpfulness benchmarking
Human eval Side-by-side preference ratings from actual users or domain experts. Gold standard. Final production validation
📈

Key training metrics to watch in DPO: train/chosen_rewards should rise, train/rejected_rewards should fall, and train/rewards/margins (chosen − rejected) should steadily increase. If margins plateau or go negative, something is wrong with your data or β value.

Complete DPO Training Script

Below is a complete, self-contained DPO training script for a Colab A100 environment using Unsloth + TRL. This takes a HuggingFace-hosted SFT model and applies DPO alignment in one pass.

Python · Full DPO pipeline 
################################################################################
# Class 23 — DPO Preference Alignment                                          
# Full-Stack GenAI Bootcamp 1.0                                                 
################################################################################

## INSTALL (run once)
# !pip install unsloth trl datasets transformers peft bitsandbytes --quiet

import torch
from unsloth import FastLanguageModel
from trl import DPOTrainer, DPOConfig
from datasets import load_dataset
from transformers import AutoTokenizer

## ── CONFIG ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SFT_MODEL_NAME   = "your-hf-username/your-sft-model"   # your Class 22 output
PREF_DATASET     = "argilla/dpo-mix-7k"
MAX_SEQ_LEN      = 2048
MAX_PROMPT_LEN   = 512
DPO_BETA         = 0.1
LORA_R           = 16
LEARNING_RATE    = 5e-5
TRAIN_EPOCHS     = 1
BATCH_SIZE       = 2
GRAD_ACCUM       = 4
OUTPUT_DIR       = "./dpo-output"
HF_PUSH_NAME     = "your-hf-username/your-model-dpo"

## ── LOAD BASE (SFT) MODEL ───────────────────────────────────────────────
model, tokenizer = FastLanguageModel.from_pretrained(
    model_name       = SFT_MODEL_NAME,
    max_seq_length   = MAX_SEQ_LEN,
    load_in_4bit     = True,
    dtype            = None,
)

## ── WRAP WITH LORA ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
model = FastLanguageModel.get_peft_model(
    model,
    r              = LORA_R,
    lora_alpha     = LORA_R,
    target_modules = ["q_proj", "k_proj", "v_proj", "o_proj",
                      "gate_proj", "up_proj", "down_proj"],
    lora_dropout   = 0,
    bias           = "none",
    use_gradient_checkpointing = "unsloth",
    random_state   = 42,
)

## ── LOAD & INSPECT DATASET ──────────────────────────────────────────────
dataset = load_dataset(PREF_DATASET, split="train")
print(f"Dataset size: {len(dataset)} preference pairs")
print(f"Columns: {dataset.column_names}")
# Expected: ['prompt', 'chosen', 'rejected']

## ── DPO TRAINING CONFIG ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
dpo_args = DPOConfig(
    beta                        = DPO_BETA,
    learning_rate               = LEARNING_RATE,
    num_train_epochs            = TRAIN_EPOCHS,
    per_device_train_batch_size = BATCH_SIZE,
    gradient_accumulation_steps = GRAD_ACCUM,
    gradient_checkpointing      = True,
    warmup_ratio                = 0.1,
    lr_scheduler_type           = "cosine",
    optim                       = "adamw_8bit",
    bf16                        = True,
    fp16                        = False,
    logging_steps               = 10,
    save_steps                  = 200,
    output_dir                  = OUTPUT_DIR,
    max_length                  = MAX_SEQ_LEN,
    max_prompt_length           = MAX_PROMPT_LEN,
    report_to                   = "none",
)

## ── TRAINER ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
trainer = DPOTrainer(
    model         = model,
    ref_model     = None,   # Unsloth manages reference internally
    args          = dpo_args,
    train_dataset = dataset,
    tokenizer     = tokenizer,
)

## ── TRAIN ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
trainer_stats = trainer.train()
print(trainer_stats)

## ── INFERENCE EXAMPLE ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
FastLanguageModel.for_inference(model)
messages = [
    {"role": "user", "content": "Explain the difference between RLHF and DPO."}
]
inputs = tokenizer.apply_chat_template(
    messages, tokenize=True,
    add_generation_prompt=True, return_tensors="pt"
).to("cuda")
outputs = model.generate(inputs, max_new_tokens=512, temperature=0.7, top_p=0.9)
print(tokenizer.decode(outputs[0], skip_special_tokens=True))

## ── SAVE & PUSH ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
model.save_pretrained("dpo_lora_adapter")
tokenizer.save_pretrained("dpo_lora_adapter")
model.push_to_hub(HF_PUSH_NAME, token="hf_...")
09 — Tips & Pitfalls

Tips, Pitfalls & Hyperparameter Reference

✅ What to Do

  • ✓ Start DPO from your SFT model, not the base model. DPO requires an instruction-following model to start with — it refines preferences, it doesn't teach instruction following from scratch.
  • ✓ Use the same chat template in DPO as in SFT. Mismatched templates cause the reference log-ratios to be computed on different token sequences, breaking the DPO loss signal.
  • ✓ Choose preference data where chosen and rejected are meaningfully different. Very similar responses (small quality gap) make it hard for DPO to learn a useful preference direction.
  • ✓ Monitor rewards/margins throughout training. A steadily growing margin (chosen reward − rejected reward) confirms that alignment is working.
  • ✓ Keep β small (0.1–0.3) to allow meaningful deviation from the SFT baseline. If the margins don't grow, try lowering β first before other changes.
  • ✓ Evaluate with a side-by-side LLM judge (GPT-4 or Claude) on a held-out eval set to confirm win-rate improvement before pushing to production.

❌ Common Pitfalls

  • ✗ Reward hacking: If β is too low or training runs too long, the model may find degenerate ways to maximise the DPO objective — for example, producing very long, verbose responses if length correlates with reward. Use length-controlled evaluation metrics (AlpacaEval 2.0) to catch this.
  • ✗ Over-refusal: Aligning on safety-heavy preference data can make the model refuse too many legitimate requests. Balance safety preferences with helpfulness preferences in your dataset mix.
  • ✗ Mode collapse on DPO: Running too many epochs can cause the model to over-concentrate probability mass on a narrow set of response styles. 1–2 epochs is usually enough.
  • ✗ Applying DPO to a base model: DPO requires the model to already understand chat format. Applying it before SFT produces garbage — the reference log-ratios are meaningless for a model that doesn't understand instructions.
  • ✗ Using a mismatched SFT reference: The ref_model in DPOTrainer should be the exact same checkpoint you're fine-tuning from. Using a different checkpoint as the reference breaks the theoretical grounding of the DPO loss.

📐 Hyperparameter Reference for DPO

Hyperparameter Recommended Value Notes
Beta (β) 0.1 – 0.3 Start at 0.1; increase if model drifts too far from SFT
Learning rate 5e-5 – 1e-4 Lower than SFT; DPO is sensitive to high LR

10 — Self-Check

Quiz — Test Your Understanding

📝 Concept Check Questions

1. Why is SFT alone insufficient for producing a fully aligned model? What fundamental capability does alignment add?

▶ Show Answer
SFT teaches the model to imitate training data by next-token prediction. It treats all correct responses as equally valid and has no mechanism to distinguish a good answer from a merely adequate one. It also cannot express a preference for safe responses over harmful ones. Alignment adds the ability to rank responses by quality — given two responses for the same prompt, the model learns to assign higher probability to the one humans prefer. This is the critical missing signal that makes the difference between a capable assistant and a trustworthy one.

2. What are the three stages of the RLHF pipeline and what does each accomplish?

▶ Show Answer
Stage 1 is SFT — training an instruction-following model as the starting policy. Stage 2 is reward model training — taking a set of (prompt, chosen, rejected) preference pairs and training a model to predict human preference as a scalar score. Stage 3 is RL fine-tuning with PPO — using the reward model as the environment signal and PPO as the RL algorithm to update the language model to maximise expected reward, with a KL divergence penalty preventing it from drifting too far from the SFT reference model.

3. What is the key mathematical insight that allows DPO to skip the reward model entirely?

▶ Show Answer
Rafailov et al. showed that the optimal RLHF policy (the solution to the KL-constrained reward maximisation problem) has a closed-form expression in terms of the reference policy and an implicit reward function. Substituting this into the Bradley-Terry preference model, you can express the preference probability directly in terms of the ratio of policy log-probabilities to reference model log-probabilities. This means you can train the language model directly on preference data with a binary cross-entropy loss — the reward model is implicitly defined by the model itself and doesn't need to be materialised separately.

4. What does the β (beta) parameter control in DPO, and what happens at the extremes (very low vs very high β)?

▶ Show Answer
Beta controls how strongly the DPO loss penalises divergence from the SFT reference model — it's the KL constraint strength. With very low β (near 0), there's almost no regularisation and the model aggressively pushes probability mass onto chosen responses, risking reward hacking, mode collapse, and catastrophic forgetting of SFT capabilities. With very high β, the model barely changes from the SFT baseline, and the preference signal has no effect. In practice, β between 0.1 and 0.3 balances learning the preference signal while preserving the SFT model's general capabilities.

5. What is reward hacking and how does the KL divergence penalty in both RLHF and DPO mitigate it?

▶ Show Answer
Reward hacking is when the model finds ways to score highly according to the reward metric without actually producing better outputs. For example, if the reward model was trained on data where longer responses tended to be preferred, the model might learn to output very long, verbose responses without adding quality. The KL divergence penalty constrains the aligned policy to stay close to the SFT reference model. Since the SFT model doesn't have these degenerate strategies, the KL penalty prevents the aligned model from finding them — it can only move in directions that are both rewarded and close to the SFT distribution.

6. What should you monitor during DPO training to verify that alignment is working, and what does each signal tell you?

▶ Show Answer
The key metrics are: (1) train/chosen_rewards — should increase, meaning the model is assigning higher log-probabilities to chosen responses relative to the reference. (2) train/rejected_rewards — should decrease, meaning the model is assigning lower log-probabilities to rejected responses relative to the reference. (3) train/rewards/margins — the difference between chosen and rejected rewards, should grow consistently throughout training. If margins plateau early, try lowering β. If they go negative, your preference data may have noise or the chosen/rejected labels may be inconsistent.

7. In what order should you apply CPT (Class 21), SFT (Class 22), and DPO (Class 23) in a production pipeline, and why does this order matter?

▶ Show Answer
The correct order is CPT → SFT → DPO. CPT (Continual Pre-Training) fills the model with domain-specific knowledge and vocabulary by training on raw text. SFT then teaches the model to express that knowledge by following instructions, using structured prompt-response pairs and chat templates. Finally, DPO teaches the model which responses are better given a choice — refining the quality and safety of its instruction-following behaviour. If you apply DPO before SFT, the model doesn't understand chat format and the preference signal is meaningless. If you skip CPT, the model may lack the domain knowledge needed to produce the correct "chosen" responses that DPO is trying to teach it to prefer.
